01 / The Mission

MISSION OVERVIEW

Artemis II is humanity's return to deep space. A 10-day crewed test flight around the Moon and back — the first time astronauts have ventured beyond low Earth orbit in over 54 years. Four astronauts will validate every system needed to eventually put boots back on the lunar surface. Read more on NASA.gov

CREWED LUNAR FLYBY

The first crewed flight of Orion and only the second flight of SLS. A free-return trajectory ensures the crew can safely return even if propulsion fails.

SYSTEMS VALIDATION

The primary objective: prove that SLS, Orion, and ground systems can safely support astronauts in deep space. Life support, navigation, communications — all tested under real conditions.

DEEP SPACE DATA

Gathering critical data on crew exposure to deep-space radiation, testing communication systems at lunar distances, and validating the heat shield at unprecedented re-entry speeds.

GATEWAY TO ARTEMIS III

This test flight paves the way for the first crewed lunar landing since 1972. Every system validated here is needed to return humans to the Moon's surface.

04 / Day by Day

MISSION TIMELINE

Day 1 — April 1
Launch & Earth Orbit
SLS launches from Kennedy Space Center Pad 39B at 6:24 PM ET. After booster and core stage separation, Orion enters a high elliptical Earth orbit and performs proximity operations with the spent ICPS upper stage.
Day 2
Trans-Lunar Injection
A critical 6-minute TLI burn using the European Service Module engine boosts velocity by ~900 mph, placing Orion on a free-return trajectory toward the Moon.
Days 3 – 5
Outbound Coast
Four-day coast toward the Moon. The crew monitors all systems, gathers data on the deep-space radiation environment, and performs minor trajectory correction burns as needed.
Day 6
Lunar Flyby
Orion passes within ~4,600 miles of the lunar surface, reaching approximately 250,000 miles from Earth. A 30–50 minute radio blackout occurs behind the Moon's far side before lunar gravity slingshots the craft back toward Earth.
Days 7 – 9
Return Journey
Orion accelerates toward Earth under gravity. Three course-correction burns fine-tune the re-entry angle to ensure a precise and safe atmospheric entry.
Day 10 — ~April 10
Re-entry & Splashdown
The fastest crewed re-entry in history at 25,000 mph. Orion uses a skip-entry technique to reduce G-forces, deploys parachutes, and splashes down in the Pacific Ocean. Navy and NASA recovery teams retrieve the crew.

Jeremy Hansen becomes the first non-U.S. citizen and first Canadian to fly beyond low Earth orbit.

09 / Questions

FAQ

Artemis II is a test flight. Its job is to prove that Orion and SLS can safely support a crew in deep space before NASA commits to a landing. The lunar landing comes on Artemis III, which will use a separate lander (SpaceX Starship HLS) to touch down on the surface.

Artemis uses modern spacecraft with vastly more capable computers, communications, and life support. Orion is larger than Apollo, carries 4 instead of 3, and uses a skip re-entry technique never tested with a crew. The mission is also more diverse and international than any Apollo flight.

A flight path that uses the Moon's gravity to swing the spacecraft back toward Earth without requiring an engine burn. If the main engine fails after TLI, the crew still comes home safely. Apollo 13 famously relied on this same principle to survive.

Returning from lunar distance means Orion falls back through Earth's gravity well at ~25,000 mph — far faster than returning from the ISS (~17,500 mph). The skip-entry technique has Orion dip into the atmosphere, bounce back up, then re-enter, spreading out the heating and G-forces.

Artemis III will be the first crewed lunar landing since 1972, targeting the Moon's south pole. Astronauts will use SpaceX's Starship Human Landing System to descend to the surface. Later Artemis missions aim to build a sustained human presence on and around the Moon.
